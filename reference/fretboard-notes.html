Notes on the fretboard

Reference · Module 0 — fretboard landmarks

The neck has 12 semitones per string, repeating at the 12th fret. Every note you need is reachable through three landmarks plus three octave shapes. Memorize those, not 130 individual fret positions.

1. Standard tuning

Strings are numbered 1–6 from the thinnest (high E) to the thickest (low E), the way chord diagrams read top-to-bottom:

string123456
open note E4B3G3D3A2E2
MIDI 645955504540

Mnemonic (high → low): Every Boy Goes Down An Elevator.

2. The two string-landmarks

Learn the natural notes on the low E (6th) and A (5th) strings first. Every barre chord you already know roots on one of these — the names are already under your fingers, you just haven't named them.

fret0135791012
6 (low E) EFGABC#DE
5 (A) ABbCDEF#GA

open / octave 5th & 7th-fret landmarks

Two anchors unlock everything. The 5th-fret note on a string = the next open string (5th fret low E = A; 5th fret A = D; etc.). The 7th-fret note = the next open string, one octave higher. That's the "5th-fret tuning trick" — and it's also how you'll project notes everywhere.

3. Octave shapes — project any note across the neck

A note's octave lives at a fixed string/fret offset, depending on the string pair. Three shapes cover everything:

Shape A — same-string + 12

Any note's octave is 12 frets up the same string.

low E, fret 3 (G) → low E, fret 15 (G, octave up)

Shape B — skip a string, up 2 frets (strings 6/5 → 4/3)

From string 6 or 5, the octave is two strings "in" and 2 frets up.

low E fret 1 (F) → D string fret 3 (F, octave up)

Shape C — skip a string, up 3 frets (to strings 2 or 1)

The B string (string 2) breaks the pattern: it's tuned a major-3rd above G (string 3), not a perfect-4th like all the others. So octaves onto string 2 or 1 jump 3 frets instead of 2.

G string fret 5 (C) → high E string fret 8 (C, octave up)

The B-string offset is the single thing that makes the guitar feel irregular. Internalize "onto string 2 or 1 = +3, anywhere else = +2" and the neck stops feeling random.

4. The 12th fret = open strings

The neck repeats at fret 12. Every open-string shape you know has an identical shape at the 12th fret, one octave up. The guitar is a 12-fret instrument that's just been copied twice.

5. Drill — name any note in 2 seconds

Do this daily for one week:

  1. Pick a random note (e.g. "G").
  2. Find it on the low E string (landmark).
  3. Use octave shapes to find every other G on the neck.
  4. Name the note one fret up and one fret down from each — that's the sharp/flat neighbor.

The interactive fretboard at /app/fretboard will show every position of any note in any key (Phase 2). Until then, this table and your guitar are the lab.
