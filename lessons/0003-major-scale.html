The major scale — music's master pattern

Every chord you know, every worship progression you'll ever play, every melody you'll ever pick out by ear — they all live inside one pattern: the major scale. Learn this pattern once and you've learned the alphabet of Western music.

The pattern: whole, whole, half, whole, whole, whole, half

A major scale is a sequence of seven notes built from a fixed recipe of intervals. Starting from any note, go:

W — W — H — W — W — W — H
(Whole tone, Whole, Half, Whole, Whole, Whole, Half)

That's it. "Whole" = 2 frets. "Half" = 1 fret. Apply this from any starting note and you get that note's major scale.

Build C major from the recipe

Start on C. Apply W-W-H-W-W-W-H:

1 (start)—C
2Whole (up 2)D
3WholeE
4Half (up 1)F
5WholeG
6WholeA
7WholeB
(octave)HalfC

Notice where the two half-steps fall: E→F (steps 3→4) and B→C (steps 7→octave). That's why there's no fret between E/F and B/C — the major-scale recipe forces it. Every major scale in every key has half-steps in the same place: between steps 3–4 and 7–1.

The half-step positions are the secret. They are why the major scale sounds like "do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-ti-do." If you internalize one thing from this lesson, internalize that steps 3→4 and 7→1 are the half-steps. Everything else is whole tones.

Build G major from the recipe

Start on G (3rd fret low E string). Apply the same W-W-H-W-W-W-H:

G → A → B → C → D → E → F# → G

Why F# and not F? Between E (step 6) and the next note, the recipe demands a whole tone. E up a whole tone = F#. This is why G major "has one sharp" — the recipe requires it.

The same pattern, anywhere on the neck

The recipe is portable. Start on any fret on any string, walk W-W-H-W-W-W-H, and you've built that major scale. This is the moveable foundation: you don't memorize 12 scales, you memorize one pattern.

Your win for today (25 minutes)

  1. Play C major on one string. On the B string (string 2), play open B, then frets 1, 3, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12. That's C major, starting from the C at fret 1. Sing "do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-ti-do" as you go.
  2. Find the half-steps. They're between frets 1→3? No — that's a whole step. Listen for where you only move one fret. (Answer: between E→F at frets 5→6, and B→C at frets 12→13.)
  3. Build D major from the recipe. Start on open D string. Walk W-W-H-W-W-W-H up that string. Where do you land for step 3? (Answer: F#. The recipe forces the sharp.)
  4. Verify on the fretboard above. Switch the key dropdown to D in the Fretboard Lab and confirm every note you played is in the scale.
