Capo strategy — put the song in your voice

The unsung hero of worship guitar is a small piece of plastic that you clamp onto a fret. With it, you can take a song you already know how to play in one key and make it sing in any key you need — without re-learning any chord shape, any scale, or any fill. Today you learn the capo: how it works, how to choose a position, and how to integrate it with everything from Modules 0–4.

What a capo does (mechanically)

A capo clamps across all six strings at a chosen fret, effectively turning that fret into a new "nut." Everything you play sounds higher by the number of semitones the capo is from the nut. A capo on fret 2 makes the guitar sound a whole step higher than normal; a capo on fret 5 makes it sound a perfect fourth higher.

The shapes you play under the capo are the same shapes you've always played. Open G chord shape under a capo on fret 5 is a C chord. Open D shape under a capo on fret 2 is an E chord. You don't relearn anything — the capo just relabels the shapes.

The single most useful fact about capos: under a capo, the chord you play is not the chord you hear. If you play a G shape with a capo on fret 2, the audience hears an A chord. The Nashville Number System (Module 2) still applies — the capo just shifts what "1" means.

Memorize one row. If you know the G shape, the rule is: capo fret = song's key minus G's fret. To play in A with G shapes, capo 2 (A is 2 semitones above G). To play in C with G shapes, capo 5. This is the entire math.

Why capos matter for your mission

Three reasons, in order of practical importance:

  1. Voice. A song is in C on the recording, but your singer is more comfortable in A. Without a capo, you'd have to re-learn every chord, every scale, every fill in A. With a capo at 5, you play the same G shapes you already know — and the audience hears the song in A. The shapes are the same; the key shifts to match the singer. This is the primary reason capos exist.
  2. Voicings and color. The same G chord shape sounds different at different capo positions — brighter at fret 7, warmer at fret 2. A capo can change the timbre of the song, not just the key.
  3. CAGED reach. Capo effectively moves the whole CAGED system up the neck. A capo on fret 5 with the G-shape position puts you in a totally different region of the fretboard than the G-shape at the nut — and your pentatonic patterns move with it. A song that calls for fills in the upper register might just need a capo.

How to choose capo position (the practical method)

Step-by-step, the way you actually do it on a Sunday morning:

  1. Find the song's key by ear (Module 3 — the progression drill in the ear trainer trains this). You hear a song, find the tonic, identify the chord numbers.
  2. Ask the singer what key they want. Most singers have a small set of keys they know a song in. If they say "A flat," you have your answer.
  3. Choose a capo position that lets you play the shapes you know. If the song is in A flat and you know G shapes, the capo needs to be on fret 1. If you don't have a capo, you learn Ab shapes. But you do have a capo.
  4. Sanity-check the CAGED position. Some capo positions push the CAGED shapes into uncomfortable regions of the neck. Capo 7 in G is technically "D" — but the position-2 pentatonic is now at fret 9, which is high and thin. If the singer can handle D in standard tuning, drop the capo and just play D shapes.
  5. Listen for the fill sweet spot. The most musical pentatonic positions for fills on acoustic sit roughly at frets 2–7. A capo that puts your G-shape position-2 there is a winner.

Capo + CAGED + pentatonic, all together

This is the part that ties Module 4 to Module 5. When you put a capo on, your pentatonic patterns move with the capo. A G major pentatonic in Position 2 (fret 3) under a capo at fret 2 becomes an A major pentatonic at the same physical position. The fretboard below the capo is now a complete fretboard in the new key, with all the same CAGED positions. The notes you play are the notes your singer hears. Nothing else changes.

The capo is the time-travel device of worship guitar. It lets you take a song you already know how to play in G and put it in any key you need. The shapes you've practiced, the motifs you've built, the CAGED positions you've memorized — all of them still work. The capo doesn't change what you know; it changes what the audience hears.

Your win for today (20 minutes)

  1. Pick a worship song you know well in G. Find the progression by ear — Module 3's progression drill trains exactly this. Confirm: is it G-D-Em-C? 1-5-6-4?
  2. Capo 2. Play the same shapes. The audience now hears A-Bm-F#m-E — the song in A. Sing or hum the melody: it sounds the same as the recording, just in a different key.
  3. Now play your pentatonic motif from Lesson 11 over the A-shape version of the same loop. The motif is the same physical position. The key sounds different. The fill still works.
  4. Find the sweet spot. Try the same song with the capo at 0, 2, 4, 5, and 7. For each, play one fill. Notice which capo position makes the fill sit best in the neck — and which matches your singer's range. That's the right answer for this song, this Sunday.
