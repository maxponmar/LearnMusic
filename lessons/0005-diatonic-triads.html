Diatonic triads — why chords belong to a key

Why does G → C → Em → D sound "right" together, but G → C → Eb → D sound wrong? Because the first four chords are all diatonic to G major — built from the same scale. This lesson shows you the seven chords that naturally live in any key.

Building a triad on each scale degree

A triad is a three-note chord: a root, a third, and a fifth. In a major key, you build seven diatonic triads — one on each scale degree — by stacking thirds using only notes from the scale.

In G major (G-A-B-C-D-E-F#), stack thirds on each degree:

1G + B + DGmajor
2A + C + EAmminor
3B + D + F#Bmminor
4C + E + GCmajor
5D + F# + ADmajor
6E + G + BEmminor
7F# + A + CF#dimdiminished
The pattern is always: Major, minor, minor, Major, Major, minor, diminished. Every major key, no exceptions. I ii iii IV V vi vii°. Roman numerals: capital = major, lowercase = minor, ° = diminished.

Why this pattern emerges (the "why," not just the "what")

It's not arbitrary — it falls out of the major scale recipe. A major triad is root + 4 frets + 7 frets (major 3rd + minor 3rd stacked). When you stack thirds on each scale degree using only scale notes, sometimes the gap is 4 frets (major), sometimes 3 (minor), and on degree 7 it's 3+3 (diminished). The W-W-H-W-W-W-H scale forces the maj-min-min-maj-maj-min-dim pattern.

The three "important" chords: 1, 4, 5

In almost every worship song (and most pop, rock, and folk), three chords carry the weight: I, IV, V — the major ones on degrees 1, 4, and 5. In G major that's G, C, D. In D major: D, G, A. In C major: C, F, G.

The vi (degree 6, minor) is the fourth most common — it gives the "emotional" minor moment. The ii (degree 2, minor) appears too. The iii is rare, and the vii° almost never appears in worship music.

Listen & play

Do this once through the key: hear → play triad → name the quality.

  1. Play G, Am, Bm, C, D, Em, F#dim in order on your guitar.

Your win for today (25 minutes)

  1. Play the seven diatonic triads of G major on your guitar: G, Am, Bm, C, D, Em, F#dim. (You know all of these except maybe Bm and F#dim — try them.)
  2. Notice the pattern as you play up the scale: bright (G), sadder (Am), sadder (Bm), bright (C), bright (D), sadder (Em), tense (F#dim), bright (G). You're hearing the I-ii-iii-IV-V-vi-vii°-I pattern.
  3. Now do the same in D major: D, Em, F#m, G, A, Bm, C#dim. Confirm the same qualities: maj, min, min, maj, maj, min, dim.
  4. Drill with the app: open the fretboard lab in D, then play the seven triad roots (D, Em, etc.) and verify each is in the scale.
