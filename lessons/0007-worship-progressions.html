Worship progressions — the patterns behind the songs

Contemporary worship is built on a handful of chord-progression patterns. Learn to recognize them by ear and you can play most modern worship songs on first hearing — without a chart, without memorizing. This is the mission-critical payoff of everything in Modules 0–1.

The five patterns that cover most contemporary worship

1. 1 – 5 – 6– – 4 (the modern worship default)

Hopeful, anthemic, resolved. The most common progression in 21st-century worship.

  • In G: G – D – Em – C
  • In D: D – A – Bm – G
  • Examples: "Forever Reign" (Hillsong), "Lord I Need You" (Matt Maher)

2. 6– – 4 – 1 – 5 (cyclic, unresolved)

Starts on the minor — feels like it wants to keep going. Great for verses that build into a chorus.

  • In G: Em – C – G – D
  • Examples: "Cornerstone" (Hillsong), "Amazing Grace (My Chains Are Gone)"

3. 1 – 4 – 5 (the three-chord foundation)

Bright, foundational, ancient. The bedrock of hymns and modern praise.

  • In G: G – C – D
  • Examples: "10,000 Reasons (Bless the Lord)", "Open the Eyes of My Heart"

4. 1 – 6– – 4 – 5 (pop-worship)

Familiar, emotionally open. Used everywhere from "Revelation Song" to the bridge of "Oceans."

  • In G: G – Em – C – D

5. 4 – 1 – 5 (plagal, restful)

The "amen" cadence extended. Restful, devotional. Often appears in pre-choruses.

  • In G: C – G – D
  • Examples: "Good Good Father", pre-chorus of "What a Beautiful Name"
The big idea: five patterns cover the vast majority of modern worship. If you can hear a song and recognize which of these five it is, you've decoded the harmony without needing a chart. The progression drill in the app trains exactly this.

How to hear a progression — the method

When a worship song comes on, do this in order:

  1. Find the key (find the "1"). Hum along until you find the note that feels like "home" — total rest. That's the tonic. The chord built on it is the 1.
  2. Hear the bass motion. Each new chord's lowest note is usually a scale degree. Is the bass going up from 1 (1→4→5) or down (1→6→4→5)? The direction is a huge clue.
  3. Match to a known pattern. Count the chords in the loop. Four chords looping? Likely 1-5-6-4 or 6-4-1-5. Three? Likely 1-4-5.
  4. Verify by playing. Try the candidate pattern in the key you found. If it sounds right, you've got it.

The "worship sus" sound

One stylistic detail: modern worship heavily uses sus4 and add9/2 chords — Dsus4, Asus4, D2, C2 — even though they don't change the Roman numeral function. A "1-5-6-4" in modern worship often sounds like G → Dsus4 → Em7 → C2. The sus/add9 coloring is the sound of contemporary worship. Same numbers, fancier voicings.

Your win for today (25 minutes)

  1. Pick a worship song you know well. Find the 1 (the home chord). Identify the next three chords. Is it 1-5-6-4? 1-4-5? Something else?
  2. Open the progression drill in the ear trainer (Ear tab → Progression mode) and do 10 rounds. The goal: reliably distinguish 1-5-6-4 from 6-4-1-5 from 1-4-5.
  3. Pick a song you've never played. Listen to the chorus. Find the 1. Guess the pattern. Try playing it. Adjust.
