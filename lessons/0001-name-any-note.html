Name any note on the fretboard

You already know the chord shapes. Today you start learning the chord names — by learning the names of the notes underneath them. This is the foundation everything else in the curriculum hangs on.

Why this matters for your mission

You want to hear a worship song and play it. That requires three skills: ears (what you heard), theory (what to call it), and fretboard (where to put your fingers). Right now your ears and theory have no way to talk to your hands, because you don't know the names of the notes you're playing. Once you can name any note on the neck, every scale, chord, and key we cover later has a physical home.

The goal isn't to memorize 130 fret positions. It's to memorize two landmarks and three octave shapes — those generate everything else.

The only two strings you need today

For this lesson, ignore four of your six strings. Just learn the natural notes on the low E (6th) and A (5th) strings. Why these two? Because every barre chord you already play roots on one of them — the note names are already under your fingers, you just haven't named them yet.

Here's the whole neck in G major — every scale note lit up. Click any dot to hear it. (In the app, this fretboard is live.)

fret0135712
6 (low E) EFGABE
5 (A) ABbCDEA

Notice the pattern: between E and F, and between B and C, there's no sharp. Every other natural note has a fret between it and the next letter. That's the whole "music alphabet": E F G A B C D E, with sharps/flats in the gaps (except E→F and B→C).

The 5th-fret anchor (the single most useful fact on the guitar)

Hold a finger on the 5th fret of any string and play that string together with the next thinner string open. They should sound the same note. That's how you tune by ear — and it tells you something profound:

The 5th-fret note on a string is the next open string.
5th fret low E = A  ·  5th fret A = D  ·  5th fret D = G

So if you know the open-string names (E-A-D-G-B-E, low to high), and you know the 5th-fret anchor, you can derive the note at any 5th-fret position without memorizing. The neck starts to make sense.

Your win for today (15 minutes)

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  1. Name every natural note on the low E and A strings, frets 0–7.
  2. Find the note G on both strings without counting up from open.
  3. Explain out loud why there's no fret between B/C and E/F.

Do this now:

  1. Pick up your guitar. Play the open low E. Say "E."
  2. Move up one fret. Say "F." (Yes, the first fret of low E is F — no sharp in between.)
  3. Keep going up, naming each note out loud, until fret 7 (B). Then do the same on the A string: A, B, C, D, E…
  4. Now test yourself: have a friend (or a random-number app) call out a letter A–G. Find it on the low E string, then on the A string. Aim for under 3 seconds per note.

When that feels easy on frets 0–7, extend to the 12th fret. Notice that fret 12 = open strings, just an octave up. The neck repeats.
