Motifs — the building block of a fill

You can play the pentatonic. You can follow a chord. You can find a CAGED position. None of that is music yet. Today you learn the move that turns scale-running into melody: the motif — a short musical idea you repeat, vary, and develop. Once you have a motif, you have something to say. Everything else is just ornament.

What a motif is

A motif is a tiny melody. Two, three, or four notes — short enough to remember in one breath, distinct enough to recognize when it returns. The ear latches onto a small figure and tracks it. The bigger the melody becomes, the more the motif earns its weight.

Think of the opening bum-bum-bum-BUM of Beethoven's Fifth Symphony. That's a motif — three notes of one rhythm, then one note of another. The whole symphony keeps repeating that figure, and the figure is the reason you can hum the piece after hearing it once.

Your worship fills work the same way. A 3-note figure played on the pentatonic, repeated with one small change, is more musical than 30 random notes from the same scale. The shape is the music, not the notes.

The big idea: a fill is a short idea you repeat, not a stream of fresh notes. Improvisation is the act of repeating almost the same idea, then changing one thing. The change is the music.

The four verbs of motif work

Once you have a motif, you have exactly four moves available. Every great lead line is a combination of them.

VerbWhat you doWhen to use it
State Play the motif exactly Opening of a fill. Introduce the idea so the ear catches it.
Repeat Play it again, identical After the first statement, immediately. Repetition is what turns notes into a melody.
Vary Change one thing — a note, a rhythm, a direction On the third or fourth time. The change is the surprise.
Develop Combine variations, extend, or fragment When you want to keep the idea going without it sounding the same.

That's the entire vocabulary. State, repeat, vary, develop. If you can do those four moves with a 3-note figure, you can solo.

Anchoring on chord tones

A motif that starts or ends on a chord tone sounds intentional. A motif that starts and ends on a chord tone sounds secure. The pattern: most of your motif can be on any pentatonic note, but the first and last note should be a note that's in the current chord.

Quick reference — the chord tones of each diatonic chord (from Module 2):

Chord (in G)Chord tones (pentatonic-safe)
G (I)G, B, D
D (V)D, F#, A
Em (vi)E, G, B
C (IV)C, E, G

G major pentatonic is G-A-B-D-E. Every chord tone above is in the pentatonic — that's the whole point of the pentatonic. So any note can work in the middle, but the first note and the last note should land on a chord tone. The middle is free.

Above: G major pentatonic, Position 2. Every brown dot (G) is a chord tone of the I chord. The other chord tones — B, D for G, F# and A for D, E and B for Em, C and E for C — are also in the pentatonic. Every fill is reachable.

Listen & play — Motif 1 (G → A → B)

Do this 5 times: click each note → play it → state → repeat → vary.

  1. Click G, A, B on the fretboard (frets 3-5-7 on string 6).
  2. Play the motif twice identically over a G chord — state, repeat.
  3. Third time: change the last note to D (G-A-D). That's vary.
  4. Move the same rhythm to the D chord, starting on fret 5 (D root).

Three motifs to steal (and break)

Here are three starting figures in G. Pick one. Play it through a G-D-Em-C loop. Don't change anything the first two times; on the third and fourth times, vary one note. That is your first fill.

Motif 1 — the rising third

G → A → B (frets 3-5-7 on string 6, low to high). State it, repeat it, then on the third pass: G → A → D (a leap up). Suddenly the figure has a climax.

Motif 2 — the bend back

B → A → G (descending, frets 7-5-3 on string 6). State it, repeat it, then on the third pass: D → A → G (start higher, drop back to G). The "bend" is the surprise.

Motif 3 — the suspension

G (long) → B (long) → A (short) → G (short). The first and last notes are long chord tones; the middle is rhythmic motion. This is the sound of a vocal-friendly fill that sits under the song rather than over it.

The hardest lesson in this curriculum: the third pass is where beginners panic and abandon the motif. Don't. Stay in the motif. The whole point is that the variation is small — one note changed, one note length swapped. If you abandon the figure, you have no music. If you stay in it, you have a tune.

Your win for today (25 minutes)

  1. Pick motif 1 (G-A-B). Loop a I-V-vi-IV in G (record yourself strumming G-D-Em-C if you don't have a backing track). Play the motif over the G chord. State it.
  2. Second time through the G chord: play the same motif, identical. Repeat. Notice the ear grabbing onto it.
  3. Third time through the G chord: vary — change the last note to a D (so the figure is G-A-D instead of G-A-B). Same rhythm, one note swapped. That is variation. That is music.
  4. Now move the motif to the next chord (D) and start it on the chord tone D. The same shape, the same rhythm — just retargeted at the new harmony. That's "follow the chord" with a motif, and it's what worship lead lines are made of.
