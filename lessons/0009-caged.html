CAGED — five shapes to play pentatonic anywhere

Lesson 8 gave you the five notes. But "the five notes" live everywhere on the neck — at every octave, on every string. You can't play a fill if you have to slide back to open position each time. Today you learn the five moveable finger patterns that put those five notes under your hand in any part of the fretboard. The system is called CAGED, and it is the single most important fretboard map a worship guitarist can own.

Where "CAGED" comes from

You already know five open chords: C, A, G, E, D. Each one is a different shape of the same kind of chord (a major triad). The insight of the CAGED system is that each of those five open-chord shapes can be moved up the neck as a barre chord — and each shape, when moved, brings its own neighborhood of pentatonic notes with it.

The name "CAGED" is both a mnemonic and the order the shapes appear as you walk up the neck from open position:

  1. C shape
  2. A shape
  3. G shape
  4. E shape
  5. D shape
  6. …and the cycle repeats one octave higher, back at C.
The big idea: CAGED names five moveable chord shapes. Each shape is also the anchor for a 5-fret pentatonic pattern. Learn the five patterns and you can locate the pentatonic in any key, anywhere on the neck, without thinking — because the same five shapes cycle forever.

The five patterns for G major pentatonic

Let's make this concrete. You're in the key of G. Here are the five CAGED positions, each a 4–5 fret window showing the pentatonic notes in that region. The brown dots are G (your root). Click any note to hear it.

Position 1 — the "G shape" (open position)

This is the pattern right at the nut. It's the one most beginners learn first because it sits in open position and needs no barre. The root sits on the open G string (string 3).

Position 2 — the "E shape" (fret 2–6)

Slide up two frets. The pattern shifts up the neck; the root is now at fret 3 on string 6 (the low E string). This is the shape that lives under every E-shape barre chord — the most-used lead position in rock and worship.

Listen & play — Positions 1 & 2

Do this 5 times per position: click every dot → play ascending → play descending.

  1. Scroll to Position 1 (open) and Position 2 (fret 3) above. Click every dot in each.
  2. Play each position slowly on your guitar — eyes closed on the third pass.
  3. In the fretboard lab, switch key to A and notice Position 2 moves up two frets — same shape.

Position 3 — the "D shape" (fret 4–8)

One more shift up. The root lands at fret 5 on string 4 (the D string) and fret 10 on string 5 — the D-shape region. This position screams "country-worship lead" — bright and twangy.

Position 4 — the "C shape" (fret 7–11)

Up again. The root is at fret 7 on string 5 (the A string). This is the C-shape neighborhood — a sweet, mid-neck zone every worship lead visits.

Position 5 — the "A shape" (fret 9–13)

One more. The root sits at fret 10 on string 6 (low E) — the A-shape region. From here the cycle wraps back to the C shape one octave up.

Don't memorize all five at once. Learn Position 1 (open G) and Position 2 (E shape at fret 3) cold — together they cover open position and the first moveable region, which is enough for 80% of worship fills. Add the others one at a time over the coming weeks. The goal is fluency, not a knowledge dump.

The one rule that makes CAGED click

Every shape is the same shape one octave apart. When you move a position up 12 frets, you land on the same notes one octave higher — so Position 1 at the nut and Position 1 at fret 12 are the same pattern. That's why the CAGED cycle is a circle, not a line: there are five shapes, then they repeat.

The practical consequence: if you can play the pentatonic in G using Position 2 (fret 3), you can play it in A using Position 2 (fret 5), in Bb using Position 2 (fret 6)… the pattern never changes, only the starting fret does. The root on string 6 tells you where to put the pattern.

Minor pentatonic uses the same shapes

Here's the final payoff. Because major and minor pentatonic share the same five notes (Lesson 8), the CAGED patterns are the same patterns — only the root moves. Position 1 of G major pentatonic (root on open G, string 3) is also Position 5 of E minor pentatonic, because E is the relative minor of G and the notes are identical. The map doesn't change; the "home" dot does.

In the root-only view above, every E is marked. Notice the dots sit in the same spots as the G major pentatonic did — the brown root just moved to E. One map, two scales. That is the entire game.

Your win for today (30 minutes)

  1. Open the Fretboard Lab → key G → Major pent. overlay. Find Position 1 (open) and Position 2 (fret 3). Play each slowly, one note at a time, ascending and descending. Do this until your fingers find the notes without your eyes.
  2. Stay in Position 2. Now switch the key to A and notice that the pattern is identical — it just started two frets higher (root now at fret 5, not 3). Play it. Same fingers, new key. That is CAGED working.
  3. Back in G, switch to Minor pent. with key E. Find the pattern at open position — it's the same dots as G major pentatonic, root now on E. Play E-G-A-B-D-E slowly. Hear the darker, bluesier color.
