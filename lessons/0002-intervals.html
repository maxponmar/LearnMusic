Intervals — the distance between two notes

An interval is the distance between two pitches, measured in semitones (frets). Every chord, every scale, every melody is built from intervals — so learning twelve of them unlocks all of music theory.

Why this matters for your mission

When you hear a worship melody and want to play it, you're hearing a sequence of intervals from the key center. When you play a G chord and an Em chord back to back, you're hearing different combinations of intervals. Theory is just naming these distances so your hands and ears can agree.

The semitone: the atom of pitch

On the guitar, a semitone = one fret. Two semitones = a whole tone = two frets. That's it. Every interval is some number of semitones:

11major 7thC → B
12octaveC → C
You already know two intervals by sound. The first two notes of "Happy Birthday" are a major 2nd (C → D, 2 frets). The first two notes of the Star Wars theme are a perfect 5th (C → G, 7 frets). Every interval has a song like this.

Two intervals you'll use every day

Two intervals are so important they have their own personality:

  • Major 3rd (4 semitones). The "bright" sound. The gap between the root and the middle note of a major chord. C → E is the emotional core of a C major chord.
  • Minor 3rd (3 semitones). The "sad" sound. The gap in a minor chord. C → Eb turns C major into C minor.

Your win for today (20 minutes)

  1. Play the open G string, then the 4th fret of the G string (B). That's a major 3rd. Sing "C… E…" against it. Hear the brightness.
  2. Now play the open G string, then the 3rd fret (Bb). That's a minor 3rd. Hear how it darkens?
  3. Play the open D string, then the A string fretted at the 2nd fret (E). Wait — that's the same major 3rd shape, just on different strings. Same interval, anywhere on the neck.
  4. Drill: pick any string, any fret. Move up 4 frets. That's always a major 3rd. Move up 3 — always a minor 3rd. Move up 7 — always a perfect 5th.

What's next

Intervals stack into scales. The major scale is a specific pattern of major 2nds and minor 2nds — and once you know that pattern, you can build a major scale in any key. That's the next lesson.
