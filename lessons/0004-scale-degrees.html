Scale degrees — hearing notes by their job

In a key, every note has a job. Some feel like home, some feel restless, some feel sad. Naming these jobs — scale degrees 1 through 7 — is the bridge between theory and playing by ear.

Why this is the most important lesson in the whole curriculum

When you hear a melody, you're not hearing random pitches. You're hearing a sequence of scale degrees relative to the key. A note that's degree 1 in G major sounds like degree 1 in D major — same job, same emotional flavor, just a different pitch. Once your ear recognizes degrees, any melody becomes a sequence you can decode and play in any key.

This is called functional ear training, and it's the method the ear trainer (in the app) actually uses. Daily practice there is where this skill gets wired in.

The seven scale degrees and their personalities

In a major key, each of the seven notes has a recognizable sound. Here they are with the personality your ear will learn to recognize:

degreenamethe sound
1tonic ("do")Home. Total rest. The note everything pulls toward.
2supertonic ("re")Restless, wants to resolve up to 3 or down to 1.
3mediant ("mi")Bright, stable. Defining "major-ness" of the key.
4subdominant ("fa")Suspended, suspended — wants to fall to 3.
5dominant ("sol")Strong, second-most-stable. A second home.
6submediant ("la")Wistful. This is the relative minor's home.
7leading tone ("ti")Tense, yearning — wants to resolve up to 1.
Start with just three: 1, 3, 5. These are the "structural" degrees — the notes of the major triad. If you can identify only degrees 1, 3, and 5 by ear, you can already pick out the skeleton of almost any melody. Add 4 next (it's distinctive — that suspended sound). Then 7 (the tense leading tone). 2 and 6 come last.

The connection to chords you already know

Here's the moment your existing chord knowledge meets theory:

  • A G major chord is built from scale degrees 1, 3, 5 of G major. (G, B, D.)
  • An Em chord is built from degrees 6, 1, 3 of G major. (E, G, B — same notes as G major but rooted on 6.) That's why Em is the "relative minor" of G — they share the same scale.
  • A C chord is built from degrees 4, 6, 1 of G major. (C, E, G.)
  • A D chord is built from degrees 5, 7, 2 of G major. (D, F#, A.)

So the progression G → C → Em → D that you've played a hundred times is really: 1 → 4 → 6 → 5 in the key of G. Same numbers in any key. This is the Nashville Number System, and it's the next module.

Your win for today (20 minutes)

  1. Open the ear trainer in the app and pick scale degrees mode: start here.
  2. Do 10 rounds. Don't worry about being fast — listen to the cadence, hum "do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-ti-do" to map the scale onto your voice, then guess.
  3. After 10 rounds, look at your accuracy. Most people start at 30–50%. That's normal. Your only goal this week: reliably identify 1, 3, and 5.
  4. Optional: play the G major scale on your guitar while singing the degree numbers out loud (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 1). This wires the names to the sound.

What's next

You now have the foundation: notes on the fretboard (Module 0), intervals, the major scale, and scale degrees (Module 1). G-C-Em-D will stop being four chord shapes and become a pattern: 1-4-6-5.
